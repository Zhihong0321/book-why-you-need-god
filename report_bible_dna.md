## 1. The 7-Day Week: Origin and Global Adoption

### 1.1 The Critical Distinction: Astronomical vs. Non-Astronomical Time Units

The year, month, and day all have direct astronomical bases:
- **Year** (~365.25 days): One complete orbit of Earth around the Sun.
- **Month** (~29.5 days): One complete cycle of the Moon's phases.
- **Day** (24 hours): One complete rotation of Earth on its axis.

**The 7-day week has NO astronomical basis whatsoever.**

As Britannica explicitly states: "Week, period of seven days, a unit of time devised with no astronomical basis." The 7-day cycle runs continuously regardless of lunar phases or seasonal events. It is purely social time -- a rhythm imposed by religion and culture, not by the cosmos.

This is not a trivial point. Every other major time unit used by human civilization derives from observable natural phenomena. The 7-day week derives from a single source: the biblical creation account (Genesis 2:2-3) and the Fourth Commandment (Exodus 20:8-11; Deuteronomy 5:12-15).

**Chinese corroboration:** China's most authoritative Chinese-language dictionary, **《辞海》 (Cihai)**, explicitly confirms this origin. Its entry states: "According to the biblical Book of Genesis, God created heaven and earth in six days, and on the seventh day He finished His work and rested. Therefore this day was honored as a holy day, called the Sabbath... The earliest people to adopt the seven-day week system were the Jewish people and early Christianity." Similarly, **百度百科 (Baidu Baike)** — the most widely used Chinese-language encyclopedia — states under its "星期日 (Sunday)" entry: "The Western division of each day of the week is determined by the Bible's account... On the seventh day, work ceased. Therefore God blessed the seventh day and called it holy." These are not Western or Christian sources; they are mainstream Chinese reference works that independently affirm the biblical origin of the 7-day week.

### 1.2 Rome's 8-Day Nundinal Cycle and the Switch to 7 Days

Before adopting the 7-day week, Rome operated on an **8-day nundinal cycle** (nundinae). The Roman Republic used this system for civil and market purposes. The transition occurred gradually during the early imperial period:

- The 7-day week entered Italy through practitioners of Eastern religions (Judaism, Christianity, astrology).
- For a time, the 8-day nundinal cycle and the 7-day week coexisted. Roman fasti (calendars) are known that show both cycles running simultaneously.
- **321 AD:** Emperor Constantine I officially established the 7-day week throughout the Roman Empire by imperial decree.

### 1.3 Constantine's Decree of 321 AD -- Exact Text

On **March 7, 321 AD**, Constantine issued what historians recognize as the first state-mandated Sunday rest law. The decree (Codex Justinianus 3.12.2/3.12.3) reads:

> "Omnes iudices, urbani plebes, et artifices, venerabili die solis quiescant. Ruri autem positi agrorum culturae libere licenterque inserviant, quoniam non alias profecto commodius pluviali aut serumen granum rivis aut vinetis scrobibus committerentur, ne occasione imperatae quietis humano generi provideri dispensatio caelestis immineret."

English translation:

> "All judges and city people and the craftsmen shall rest upon the venerable day of the sun. Country people, however, may freely attend to the cultivation of the fields, because it frequently happens that no other days are better adapted for planting the grain in the furrows or the vines in trenches. So that the advantage given by heavenly providence may not for the occasion of a short time perish."

**Key observations:**
- Constantine used the pagan term **"dies solis"** (day of the sun), not the Christian "Lord's Day." This was deliberate -- Gibbon notes Constantine "styles the Lord's Day Dies Solis, a name which could not offend the ears of his pagan subjects."
- The law applied to cities, judges, and craftsmen. **Farmers were explicitly excluded** -- a critical contrast to the biblical Sabbath, which even applied during planting and harvest seasons (Exodus 34:21).
- The decree did not mandate worship; it mandated rest.
- Constantine was still minting coins honoring Sol Invictus as late as the 320s.

Despite its pagan framing, this law set the precedent that would spread globally. About 50 years after Constantine's death, Theodosius I and later emperors enacted Sunday rest laws with added Christian meaning.

### 1.4 Soviet Union: The Failed Attempt to Abolish the 7-Day Week

The Soviet experiment provides powerful negative confirmation of the 7-day week's cultural necessity.

**1929: The Nepreryvka (Continuous 5-Day Work Week)**
- In 1929, Bolshevik economist **Yuri Larin** proposed the *nepreryvka* -- a "continuous work week."
- The workforce was divided into five groups. Each worker labored four days and rested one day.
- The staggered schedule meant **80% of the workforce was always on duty**.
- Days were color-coded (yellow, peach, red, purple, green) or marked with symbols (wheatsheaf, red star, hammer and sickle, book, budenovka).
- There was no common rest day. Families, friends, and spouses rarely had the same day off.

**What happened:**
- Workers wrote letters to *Pravda*: "What is there for us to do at home if our wives are in the factory, our children at school and nobody can visit us? It is no holiday if you have to have it alone."
- Absenteeism shot up. Machines broke down from constant use without maintenance windows.
- Religious observance was intentionally disrupted -- without a Friday, Saturday, or Sunday, Muslims, Jews, and Christians alike could not attend services.
- Sociologist Eviatar Zerubavel notes: temporal symmetry (shared schedules) is "the glue that holds society together. Here, there was no common rest."

**1931: The 6-Day Week Attempt**
- By the end of 1931, the Soviet government acknowledged failure. The cycle was extended to six days, with a common day off restored.
- Stalin himself called the original decision hasty and noted the lack of personal responsibility it created.

**June 26, 1940: Forced Reversion to 7-Day Week**
- A Decree of the Presidium of the Supreme Soviet **formally reinstated the seven-day cycle**.
- The nepreryvka had lasted roughly 11 years.
- The reversion came with a cost: under the same decree, being late by more than 20 minutes, missing work, or quitting became criminal offenses with mandatory prison sentences.

**Why it failed:**
1. **Social cohesion collapsed** without shared rest.
2. **Productivity fell** -- machines broke, absenteeism rose, shared responsibility meant no responsibility.
3. **Human rhythm** -- the 7-day cycle is deeply embedded in biological, social, and religious patterns.
4. **The Soviet government was fighting human nature** -- and lost.

### 1.5 China's Adoption of the 7-Day Week

**Traditional Chinese System: The 旬 (Xun) -- 10-Day Weeks**

Before Western contact, China used a **10-day cycle** called the 旬 (*xun*). Each lunar month was divided into three xun:
- 上旬 (first xun): days 1-10
- 中旬 (second xun): days 11-20
- 下旬 (third xun): remaining days

During the **Han dynasty**, officials were legally required to rest every five days (twice per xun, or 5-6 times per month). This was not a universal rest for all people -- it applied primarily to government officials.

**Contact and Naming**
- The Christian 7-day week was known in China as early as the 16th-18th centuries through Jesuit contact.
- It became widely familiar in the **19th century** with Western traders and missionaries.
- Chinese observed that Westerners worshipped every seven days. The term **礼拜 / 禮拜 (*libai*)**, meaning "worship," was extended to mean "week." The first written record appears in **Robert Morrison's (马礼逊) *A Dictionary of the Chinese Language* (1828)**, which notes: "WEEK of seven days is called in Canton, 一个礼拜. FRIDAY, they call at Canton 礼拜五日." Morrison (1782–1834) was the first Protestant missionary to China.
- Chinese linguist **刘曼 (Liu Man)**, in her paper **"'礼拜'来源再考" (A Re-examination of the Origin of 'Libai')"** published in the peer-reviewed journal **《国际汉学》 (International Sinology, 2021)**, traces the full etymology: the word "礼拜" was first coined by Spanish Dominican missionaries in the Philippines in the late Ming period, entered Hokkien (Minnan) Chinese through Spanish-Chinese language contact, and later spread to Cantonese and Mandarin via Protestant missionaries like Morrison.
- **Sunday = 礼拜天 (*libaitian*) = "day of worship."** The other days were numbered from it: Monday = "worship plus one," etc.

**Official Adoption**
- **1902:** The Qing government began formally implementing the seven-day week system nationwide, according to Chinese historical records (中国新闻网《古代放休假制度》). Prior to this, figures like **张之洞 (Zhang Zhidong)** had explicitly forbidden schools like 两湖书院 from adopting the Western week, viewing it as foreign and religious.
- **1909:** **袁嘉谷 (Yuan Jiagu, 1872–1937)**, a distinguished scholar appointed head of the 学部编译图书局 (Ministry of Education's Compilation and Translation Bureau), personally supervised the standardization of textbook terminology. According to 百度百科, it was under Yuan's leadership that "the seven-day week system was established as China's own '星期' (xingqi)."
- **February 10, 1912:** Shortly after the fall of the Qing dynasty, the new Republican government officially adopted the Western week in the government gazette (《南京临时政府公报》), using the term **星期 (*xingqi*)**.
- *Xingqi* literally means "star period" -- a deliberate attempt to find a secular, classical-sounding term to replace *libai* (which had Christian associations).
- The numbering system from *libai* was kept intact; only the word for "week" changed. Sunday became 星期日/星期天 (*xingqiri/xingqitian*) -- "star period day," replacing the explicit "day of worship."
- Academic **张书华 (Zhang Shuhua)** has documented this transmission in detail in his paper **"西制东渐：试探'星期制'于清末上海之传布" (Western Institutions Moving East: The Spread of the Week System in Late Qing Shanghai)**.

**PRC Era and Saturday/Sunday Rest Days**
- *Xingqi* gained dominance after the founding of the People's Republic of China in **1949**.
- The two-day weekend was **not** standard in early Communist China.
- On **March 25, 1995**, Premier **Li Peng** signed **State Council Order No. 174**, mandating a **five-day, 40-hour work week**, effective **May 1, 1995**.
- This made Saturday and Sunday the official rest days for the nation.

**Qing Dynasty Work Schedule (Pre-1912)**
- Under the Qing dynasty, ordinary farmers worked according to agricultural seasons -- there was no weekly rest day.
- Qing officials followed traditional Han-era patterns of rest every 5-10 days.
- Emperor Yongzheng (reigned 1722-1735) was legendary for his work ethic: he reportedly slept only four hours per night and granted himself only **three holidays per year** (Spring Festival, Winter Solstice, and his birthday).
- There was no concept of a weekly day of rest for the general population. The very idea was foreign.

## 2. Ten Commandments to Modern Law

The Ten Commandments (Exodus 20; Deuteronomy 5) are not merely religious rules. They are the oldest surviving codified legal code that explicitly addresses murder, theft, perjury, property rights, family structure, and labor protection in a single integrated framework. Below is the trace from each commandment to its modern legal descendant.

These are not Christian apologetics sources; they are a Chinese academic journal and a Chinese government judicial website.

### 2.1 "You Shall Not Murder" -> Criminal Homicide Law

**Legal descendant:** Murder statutes, manslaughter laws, and the universal criminalization of unjustified killing.

Every civilized legal system criminalizes homicide. The Code of Hammurabi (c. 1750 BC) and other ancient codes addressed killing, but the Mosaic formulation is distinct in two ways:
1. It applies to all persons equally -- not just nobles, citizens, or free men.
2. It is framed as an absolute moral prohibition, not merely a property crime against the victim's owner or family.

**Explicit citations:**
- English Common Law homicide statutes trace their moral foundation through the biblical prohibition.
- The Supreme Court of the United States, in multiple decisions, has acknowledged that prohibitions on murder are foundational to ordered liberty and pre-exist positive law.

### 2.2 "You Shall Not Steal" -> Property Law

**Legal descendant:** Theft statutes, larceny, burglary, robbery, embezzlement, fraud, and the entire edifice of property rights.

Property law in Western tradition rests on the premise that individuals can own and exclude others from material goods. The biblical prohibition against theft undergirds:
- Anglo-American property law
- Contract law (breach = taking what is not yours)
- Intellectual property concepts (though these are more modern extensions)

### 2.3 "You Shall Not Give False Testimony" -> Perjury Law and Rules of Evidence

**Legal descendant:** Perjury statutes, oath requirements in court, rules against suborning perjury, and the entire adversarial system's reliance on truthful testimony.

This commandment is the direct ancestor of:
- Courtroom oaths ("Do you swear to tell the truth...")
- Perjury as a felony offense
- Rules of evidence excluding hearsay and requiring firsthand testimony
- Contempt of court for false statements

The biblical text specifically places this prohibition in a judicial context -- "against your neighbor" -- indicating it was meant for legal proceedings, not merely casual lying.

### 2.4 "You Shall Not Covet" -> Criminal Intent (Mens Rea)

**Legal descendant:** The concept of *mens rea* (guilty mind), conspiracy laws, intent requirements in criminal statutes, and prohibitions on stalking and harassment.

This is the most legally sophisticated commandment. While Western law does not criminalize mere desire, it criminalizes:
- **Conspiracy** (agreeing to commit a crime)
- **Attempt** (taking substantial steps toward a crime)
- **Stalking and harassment** (where the mental fixation on another person generates harmful action)
- **Fraud and insider trading** (where wrongful desire for another's property generates deceptive action)

As legal scholar Akhil Reed Amar has noted, the Tenth Commandment regulates the *interior state* that precedes theft and adultery -- a remarkable early recognition that law must address intent as well as action.

### 2.5 "Honor Your Father and Your Mother" -> Family Law and Inheritance

**Legal descendant:** Family law (parental rights, child support, elder care obligations), inheritance law, and statutory duties to dependents.

- **Inheritance statutes** in Common Law historically protected lineal descent, reflecting the biblical priority of family continuity.
- **Elder abuse laws** and filial responsibility statutes in multiple U.S. states and civil law jurisdictions directly reflect this commandment.

## 3. Mosaic Law to Western Legal Tradition

### 3.1 Mosaic Law and Roman Law

Direct channels from Mosaic law to Roman law are debated among historians. However, several connections are well-established:

1. **Jewish communities in the Roman Empire** practiced their own legal system (*halakha*) under Roman toleration. Roman jurists were aware of Mosaic law.
2. **The early Church**, which became the dominant religion of the Empire, carried biblical legal concepts into Roman canon law.
3. **The Justinian Code** (6th century AD) was compiled by Christian jurists who operated within a biblical worldview, even when drawing on classical Roman sources.
4. **Natural law theory** -- the idea that law transcends human legislation -- was developed by Christian thinkers (Thomas Aquinas, etc.) who explicitly grounded it in biblical revelation.

### 3.2 English Common Law: Blackstone and Biblical Foundations

**Sir William Blackstone** (1723-1780), whose *Commentaries on the Laws of England* (1765-1769) became the definitive treatise on English Common Law, is explicit about the Bible's foundational role.

In Book I, Chapter 1, Section 2 ("Of the Nature of Laws in General"), Blackstone writes:

> "This will of his maker is called the **law of nature**. For as God, when he created matter, and endued it with a principle of mobility, established certain rules for the perpetual direction of that motion; so, when he created man, and endued him with free will to conduct himself in all parts of life, he laid down certain immutable laws of human nature, whereby that free will is in some degree regulated and restrained, and gave him also the faculty of reason to discover the purport of those laws."

Blackstone continues:

> "This law of nature, being coeval with mankind and **dictated by God himself**, is of course superior in obligation to any other. It is binding over all the globe in all countries, and at all times: **no human laws are of any validity, if contrary to this**; and such of them as are valid derive all their force, and all their authority, mediately or immediately, from this original."

And most directly:

> "The doctrines thus delivered we call the **revealed or divine law**, and they are to be found only in the holy Scriptures... Upon these **two foundations, the law of nature and the law of revelation**, depend all human laws; that is to say, no human law should be suffered to contradict these."

Blackstone explicitly identifies the "revealed or divine law" with the Bible and states that it is one of the two foundations upon which all valid human law rests.

## 4. Sabbath: The World's First Labor Protection Law

### 4.1 The Ancient World: No Mandatory Rest

Before the Mosaic Sabbath commandment (c. 15th-13th century BC, traditionally dated to the Exodus), no civilization in the ancient world mandated a regular day of rest for:
- Slaves
- Servants
- Animals
- Foreigners/sojourners

**Ancient Egypt:**
- Slaves worked continuously under the pharaoh's building projects.
- The biblical narrative explicitly contrasts the Sabbath with Egyptian slavery: "Remember that you were a slave in the land of Egypt and the LORD your God brought you out with a mighty hand and an outstretched arm. Therefore the LORD your God commanded you to keep the Sabbath day" (Deuteronomy 5:15).

**Mesopotamia:**
- The Code of Hammurabi (c. 1750 BC) regulates slavery, trade, and violence -- but contains no weekly rest requirement.
- The Code of Ur-Nammu (c. 2100-2050 BC), the oldest known law code, addresses slaves -- but does not mandate rest.

**Greece and Rome:**
- Classical Greek and Roman slavery was total. Slaves had no legal personhood and no right to time.
- The Roman 8-day nundinal cycle included market days but did not mandate rest for workers or slaves.
- Roman law treated slaves as property (*res*), not persons with rights.

### 4.2 The Biblical Sabbath: Unprecedented Scope

The Fourth Commandment (Exodus 20:8-11; Deuteronomy 5:12-15) mandates rest for:
- The free citizen
- His sons and daughters
- His male servant
- His female servant
- His livestock (ox, donkey, animals)
- The foreigner residing in his gates

This was revolutionary. No previous legal code had:
1. **Mandated rest for slaves.** Slaves were legally entitled to the same day of rest as their masters.
2. **Mandated rest for animals.** The Sabbath recognized that beasts of burden also need rest.
3. **Mandated rest for foreigners.** Non-citizens were included in the rest requirement.

In effect, the Sabbath was the **first labor protection law in human history** -- and it protected the lowest members of society.

### 4.3 Modern Labor Law: Direct Lineage to the Sabbath

The 40-hour work week and the two-day weekend have direct lineage to the Sabbath principle:

1. **Constantine's 321 AD decree** established Sunday rest as civil law in the Roman Empire. This precedent spread across Europe.
2. **Medieval Europe** operated on a rhythm of feast days, Sundays, and religious holidays -- creating the first widespread weekly rest pattern outside Israel.
3. **The Jewish Sabbath (Saturday)** created friction in early industrial factories. By the early 20th century, some American factories began closing on **both Saturday and Sunday** to accommodate Jewish workers -- creating the modern "weekend."
4. **The 40-hour, 5-day work week** (standardized in the U.S. by the Fair Labor Standards Act of 1938 and in China by State Council Order No. 174 in 1995) is the secular institutionalization of the biblical rhythm: **six days of labor, one or two days of rest.**

The International Labour Organization (ILO) recognizes weekly rest as a fundamental labor right. The biblical Sabbath is its oldest known source.

**Chinese scholarly perspective:** **刘平 (Liu Ping)**, Professor of Philosophy at **复旦大学 (Fudan University)**, one of China's top universities, published **"圣化时间：超越现代性的时间建筑学——海舍尔安息日思想述评" (Sanctifying Time: A Review of Heschel's Sabbath Thought, 2006)**, which won the Shanghai Social Sciences Association Annual Outstanding Paper Award. Liu explicates the Sabbath's threefold meaning in the Hebrew tradition: (1) it is a day blessed and sanctified by God; (2) it embodies a paradoxical freedom — celebrating both God's sovereignty in creation and human freedom; (3) it establishes a rhythm of work and rest that predates and transcends all secular labor legislation. That a professor at a secular Chinese university would publish award-winning research on Sabbath theology underscores the universal significance of this biblical institution.

## 6. Specific China Data Points

### 6.1 Qing Dynasty Work Schedule

Under the Qing dynasty (1644-1912), there was **no weekly day of rest for ordinary people**:

- **Farmers:** Worked according to agricultural seasons. The concept of a regular day off did not exist. Work was determined by planting, tending, and harvest cycles.
- **Officials:** Followed the traditional pattern inherited from the Han dynasty -- rest every 5-10 days (within the 旬 *xun* system). This was a privilege of office, not a universal right.
- **Emperor Yongzheng** was famous for extreme diligence, reportedly sleeping only four hours per night and taking only **three holidays per year.**
- The Qing calendar was lunisolar. Days were tracked within 10-day *xun* cycles. There were no named "weekdays."

### 6.2 Entry of the 7-Day Week into China

- The Western 7-day week was known in China from **Jesuit contact** (16th-18th centuries).
- It became widespread in the **19th century** through Western traders, diplomats, and missionaries.
- The Chinese term **礼拜 (*libai*)** -- meaning "worship" -- was applied to the week because Chinese observers noticed Westerners worshipped every seven days.

### 6.3 Chinese Scholar Acknowledging the Origin

**Yuan Jiagu (袁嘉谷, 1872-1937)**, an outstanding scholar of the late Qing/early Republican period, is explicitly remembered for setting up a government department to supervise terminology in textbooks (1909) and for supporting the adoption of **星期 (*xingqi*)** as the official term for the 7-day week in the 1912 government gazette.

The government's substitution of *xingqi* for *libai* was a deliberate act of linguistic secularization — changing the name while keeping the foreign institution.
